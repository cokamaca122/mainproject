Conclusion

As I continue my research, I find myself stepping outside my usual mindset to explore different thoughts and ideas. This course, along with the material presented in the mind map on this page, supports that growth and encourages me to engage more with another field.

Even though my mind map focuses on qualitative research, I am not suggesting that qualitative research is better or superior, nor do I see the two as separate or opposing. As discussed in the first section, based on Mason’s work, there is no clear divide between qualitative and quantitative research. By making this mind map web page, as well as exploring the course literature and discussions, I have learned the importance of avoiding a dualistic view - qualitative versus quantitative. This also applies to other dualities we discussed that often appear in academic and clinical contexts, such as disability versus normality, or science versus art. These simplified divisions can limit our understanding and hinder our scientific journey. In my research with older adults, I have seen how these categories can overlook the complexity of people's experiences. Many participants might not identify strictly with the labels given to them. Furthermore, I also saw how music, which is often seen as part of the arts, can play a role in health and rehabilitation as well as the contribution to science and research.

While participating in music sessions in the inpatient unit, I began questioning whether the questionnaires I had been using (at the outpatient unit) would be an appropriate data collection method. It made me reflect on what it means to include participants in research and how we, as researchers, should not only choose participants who fit our methods but also adapt our approaches to include the participants who may be excluded from the research. This course helped me explore possible ways of doing so, particularly through flexible and creative qualitative methods that can better support inclusion and equity in research.

Through this course and the process of creating the mind map, I have also gained a better understanding of the complexity of defining disability. These definitions can influence not only healthcare and rehabilitation practices, but also how participants experience and express their identities. This is something I will continue to reflect on and integrate into my future research.

After completing this course and the mind map website, I feel better equipped to continue my research. I am now more aware of the aspects of research I had not encountered before. While there is still much more to learn, I have a solid foundation to build on. With this experience, I will not approach my current or future research in the same way. I will be able to explore more options and make more informed decisions about designing and carrying out a study while continuing to develop my participant-centred approach to research.
