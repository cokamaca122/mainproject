Online Music Study

  1. Pre-study Meeting with Program Staff

    Usually, before the research study starts, I meet with employees who are in charge of all relevant programs. They organize the schedule and coordinate the programs. During this meeting, we discuss logistics, research duration, and the overall plan—addressing details such as group demographics (e.g., size, general health, age) and the study’s feasibility. We also explore any potential difficulties.

    In my opinion, this meeting could be easily conducted online via conferencing platforms like Zoom. It would also cover details regarding the room or space where the study takes place, as well as technical aspects (e.g., screen/monitor size, keyboard, mouse, webcam, speakers, and sound volume). For example, I know that my current research facility offers a screen that is already used for activities and programs, and we could even discuss having a technical person available in the room for immediate troubleshooting.

  2. Assessing Participants’ Comfort with Technology

    Before the study begins, I should explore participants’ comfort levels with technology. I could provide training, tutorials, and periodic check-ins or follow-up sessions via Zoom to help address any difficulties they might experience.

  3. Distributing and Reviewing Consent Forms

    Consent forms can be distributed using third-party applications that protect privacy (such as REDCap or DocuSign) or via email. Participants should have about a week to review the forms and decide on participation, giving them ample time to ask questions. If needed, assistance can be provided by caregivers, family members, or staff. In my study, staff have been actively involved in every step of organization and coordination.

  4. Program Presentation and Q&A Session

    After one week, the music teacher and I typically present the program and research study to the participants. The teacher leads a short demo class, and I share detailed information about the study and answer any questions. In my opinion, this process could also be conducted via Zoom or another remote conferencing platform, since the teacher and I do not need to be in the same location.

  5. Ensuring Consistency in Technology

    Consistency in technology is crucial to avoid confusion. REDCap may be the ideal solution since it runs on almost any platform while unifying many research aspects. Participants should be provided with tablets to complete questionnaires and use the platform exclusively. In my study, tablets and a specialized platform from the University of Ottawa are available—but this requires in-person distribution or shipping of devices, plus tutorials or online sessions to familiarize everyone with the technology. Additionally, privacy concerns (e.g., the ability to disable a lost tablet) and ethical guidelines must be thoroughly considered.
