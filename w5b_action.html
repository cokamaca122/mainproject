Ethics in Action: Case Examples

  1. Ensuring Anonymity During Data Collection

    Challenge: As I was conducting an info session for my study, some participants raised concerns about keeping their data private. They refused to participate, since they did not want to write their names on the questionnaires. However, this was a study requirement.

    Solution: I introduced a system that allowed participants to use pseudonyms and place their questionnaires in a secured box. The plan was to use an approach similar to voting, in order to keep the data confidential. This approach resolved the participants' concerns and fulfilled the study's requirements.

    Further Reflections: This participant-centred example shows how adapting methods can effectively address anonymity concerns, maintain research integrity, and build trust between participants and researchers.

    Challenge: During a testing session, I noticed a participant with hand tremors struggling to turn pages of a questionnaire. In addition, they refused the help I offered. It was clear to me that I would have to find another solution.

    Solution: I placed colourful paper clips on the pages, which allowed the participant to turn pages more easily without my help. In this way, I respected their autonomy while encouraging their participation in the study.

    Further Reflections: This example shows the need for accessibility and accommodations in research settings. Making adjustments that respect participants' independence and unique needs is particularly relevant in studies on rehabilitation and disability, where capturing authentic experiences is crucial.

  3. Maintaining Privacy in Research Settings

    Challenge: While I was conducting a Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) with a vulnerable participant in a small, multipurpose room, the testing was interrupted by a staff member.

    Solution: I immediately paused the examination. The participant's privacy and comfort were the most important aspects at that moment. While we waited for the staff to leave, I used calming facial expressions and reassuring body language and spoke softly to keep the participant patient and prevent further agitation.

    Further Reflections: Conducting research in a secure and private environment is essential. As researchers, we need to make sure participants feel safe and that the integrity of the data collected is not compromised in any way. It is also about doing our best in an unexpected situation to make participants feel safe and respected, especially when working with people facing mental health challenges.
