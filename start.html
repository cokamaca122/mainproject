Introduction to Qualitative Research in Rehabilitation and Disability Studies

As a piano and ear training teacher, I am currently pursuing a master’s degree in music. My interest in research began with a course on research methodologies, which led to my involvement in a project exploring music and movement with older adults in a mental health care setting. This experience sparked my curiosity about the relationship between music and health, and I was given the opportunity to continue contributing to the project by working as a research assistant.

As I progressed, I realized how much there is to learn; not only about research itself but also about the context in which it takes place and the populations that are involved. Taking the course Qualitative Research in Rehabilitation and Disability Studies helped me expand my thinking about how qualitative research can be applied in fields like rehabilitation and disability studies. While my current research focuses on older adults, I remain open to expanding my research to other populations.

I created a mind map to introduce key ideas in qualitative research for students in rehabilitation and disability, highlighting major concepts, ethical considerations, and analytical approaches. While reflecting on my perspective as a student, this mind map represents my exploration of qualitative research in the context of rehabilitation and disability, connected to my experience with a music and movement program for older adults in a mental health care setting.



How to Use the Mind Map

1. Start with any main section — such as Foundations, Qualitative Methodologies, or other sections marked on the map.

2. Begin by reading the introduction for that section. Each main section includes a short description of the content.

3. Explore the materials by following the map arrows that are the same colour as the main section. These arrows connect the main topic to its subsections, which include videos, examples, quotes, or visuals.

4. Once you have explored all the content in subsections, return to the main section and click on the marked area to view the conclusion for that section.

5. Repeat this process for each main section until you reach the final “Conclusion” at the bottom of the map.
